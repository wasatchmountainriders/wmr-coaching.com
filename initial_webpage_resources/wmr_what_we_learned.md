# Wasatch Mountain Riders — Curriculum Reference

A reference document capturing the thinking behind the WMR skill card system. Useful for explaining program decisions to parents, onboarding new program directors, or coming back later to understand why things are structured the way they are.

---

## The system

Three layers of resources, each serving a different moment in a coach's workflow:

1. **Index page** — landing page tying everything together
2. **Skill cards** — 7 program decks, 12 cards each (84 total), one per skill. Editorial design, read pre-season.
3. **Universal reference** — Protocols, Stuck, Readiness, Standards. Single source of truth for emergency procedures, conduct standards, and pre-ride checklists.

---

## The seven programs

| Program | Ages | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Lil' Shredders (coed) | 5–7 | First-time riders. Fundamentals taught as games. |
| Beginner Boys | 7–9 | The bridge from playing on bikes to mountain biking. |
| Beginner Girls | 7–9 | Same skills, capability-first framing. |
| Intermediate Boys | 8–11 | Real technique. Bike-body separation, switchbacks, drops, tabletops. |
| Intermediate Girls | 8–11 | Same skills, PHV-aware coaching, role models built in. |
| Advanced Boys | 9–12 | Race-pace technique. J-hops, manuals, trail-braking, risk discipline. |
| Advanced Girls | 9–12 | Same skills. Retention is the explicit goal. |

The gender split exists from age 7+. The skills are identical; the coaching cues are calibrated for what works for each group at each age.

## The developmental framework

Skills were sequenced by what each age can actually do — not by what looks impressive. The core developmental windows informing the curriculum:

### Ages 5–7 (Lil' Shredders): "FUNdamentals"

Pre-skill window. Kids are learning to *ride the bike at all.* Motor patterns are forming but coordination is unreliable. Fear is physical (their vestibular system is still developing). Attention spans are short. Games disguise practice better than drills.

What works:
- **Ready position ("ninja stance")** as the base posture — static, simple
- **Falling safely** taught Day 1 on grass (note: not in formal NICA curriculum but defensible developmentally)
- **Game-based teaching** — Statue Stance, Red Light Green Light, Slow Bike Race
- **Cues under 10 words**
- **No expectation of consistent execution**

What doesn't work:
- Complex multi-step instructions
- Speed-based challenges (creates panic)
- Forcing kids through fear (it imprints, badly)

### Ages 7–9 (Beginner): "Learn to Train" window opens

The motor learning window starts opening. Kids can finally hold a single technique cue across reps. They can think about cause and effect briefly. Strength is largely undifferentiated by sex — girls and boys at this age can do exactly the same things on a bike.

What works:
- **Attack position** (dynamic, hip-hinged — upgrade from "ready position")
- **Eye discipline** as an explicit skill
- **Real drills** alongside games (Stop Box, Cone Carve, Slow Bike Race)
- **Front wheel lift** as the first "trick" — extremely motivating

Key principle: **Boys and girls in this age group are physically the same.** Treat skills identically. Calibrate the *coaching language* (boys often respond to challenge framing; girls often respond to capability framing) but the skills themselves are identical.

### Ages 8–11 (Intermediate): Prime motor-learning window

This is the most important coordination-skill window of their lives. Skills practiced now consolidate deeply for life. Strength is still mostly neural (technique) rather than muscular (power). Most are still pre-PHV (peak height velocity); some late-stage 10s and 11s are entering PHV.

What works:
- **Bike-body separation** — the mechanical unlock
- **Switchbacks** — combining slow balance, eye discipline, body separation
- **American bunny hop** — both wheels off, flat pedals only
- **Trail-braking introduced** but not as own card yet
- **Real obstacles** — drops, tabletops, technical climbing/descending

PHV awareness becomes critical here, especially for girls (girls hit PHV ~11.5 vs boys ~13). A 10-year-old who was crushing it in spring may suddenly look clumsy in summer — they grew. Don't punish it. Acknowledge it.

### Ages 9–12 (Advanced): Closing the window

Upper end of "Learn to Train." Many late-stage Advanced riders are mid- or post-PHV. Strength gains are starting to matter alongside technique. Risk tolerance is way up. Some are starting to think about racing.

What works:
- **Attack position under load** (not just "can you do it" but "can you hold it tired")
- **Trail-braking as its own skill**
- **J-hop** (the real bunny hop) and **manuals**
- **Race-day fundamentals** (pacing, starts, passing)
- **Mental skills and risk assessment** as foundational

The most important card in the Advanced decks is the last one — Mental skills, risk, and retention. The Advanced years are make-or-break for whether they stay riders for life.

## Gender-specific coaching: what we learned

The skills are identical. The coaching language is different. Here's what matters:

### Boys (7–12)

**What works:**
- Competitive framings ("Show me you can do it")
- Pro name-checks for aspiration (Christopher Blevins, Riley Amos, Tom Pidcock)
- Channeling natural risk-taking into technique discipline ("send it cleanly first, then add speed")
- Direct correction of social dynamics (one-upmanship, mocking)
- Acknowledging that "skid to win" is easy and "control" is the actual skill

**What to watch for:**
- Fake confidence ("yeah, I got it" without conviction) — boys often hide fear
- One-upmanship on drops and jumps — real safety issue
- Mashing in hard gears to look strong (vs. spinning in easy gears like pros)
- Growth-spurt coordination dips at late-stage 11–12 — temporary, not regression

### Girls (7–12)

**What works:**
- Capability-first framing from card one ("you belong here")
- Female pro name-checks (Haley Batten, Puck Pieterse, Pauline Ferrand-Prévot, Evie Richards)
- Personal records ("can you string 5 in a row?") instead of head-to-head competition
- Matter-of-fact handling of body changes (periods, saddle fit, padded shorts)
- Naming and shutting down comparison-to-boys trap
- "You are the proof this sport belongs to her" — for female coaches especially

**What to watch for:**
- Quietly anxious (withdrawal rather than bravado) — girls often hide fear by going quiet
- "I can't" before trying — common verbalization at this age
- Holding back from features she's actually ready for (different from real fear)
- Body comments from anyone — even well-intentioned ones cause harm
- The retention cliff at 11–14 (see below)

### The critical retention research

**Girls drop out of cycling at much higher rates than boys between ages 11 and 14.** The documented drivers:

- Being pushed through fear creates lifelong avoidance
- Being shamed for walking features kills belonging
- Comparison to boys kills belonging
- Comments on bodies (even well-intentioned) drive girls out of sports
- Tone-deaf handling of periods or body changes
- Cultural pressure from outside the program

The Advanced Girls Mental & Risk card names this explicitly. The retention message is: **the goal isn't to make her a pro. The goal isn't to make her win races. The goal is to keep her riding — for joy, for fitness, for community, for life.**

## The drill name standardization

All 7 decks use a consistent drill vocabulary. Drills marked with † appear in multiple decks intentionally — same drill name, getting more demanding with age.

### Lil' Shredders (5–7)
Statue Stance, Bump Hunting, The Log Crossing, Coaster Coast, Cone Carve†, Pump Track Laps†, Caterpillar Ride†

### Beginner Boys & Girls (7–9) — identical drill names
Rolling Attack, Stop Box, Slow Bike Race, Call & Find, Cone Carve†, Pump Track Laps†, Pre-Shift Climbs, Descent Progression, Roll the Log, Front Wheel Lift over a Stick, No-Pedal Pump Track, Caterpillar Ride†

### Intermediate Boys & Girls (8–11) — identical drill names
Attack Holds, Three-Stop Progression, Bar Push Drill, Carve & Exit, Cone Switchback, Climb Reading, Tech Descent Progression, Hop the Stick, Drop Progression, Pump Track Speed Test, Tabletop Progression, A/B Line Practice

### Advanced Boys & Girls (9–12) — identical drill names
Fatigued Attack, Late Brake Marker, Apex Selection, Trail Switchback Sessions, Drop Repetition, J-Hop Progression, Manual Progression, Tabletop Refinement, Tech Descent Progression, Climbing Sets, Race Simulation, Pre-Ride Risk Check

### Naming principle

Drill names should describe what the kid is *doing*, not just what skill is being taught. "Statue Stance" works for 5-year-olds (they freeze) but "Rolling Attack" describes what 8-year-olds actually do (hold position while coasting). The verb in the drill name should match the action on the bike.

Also: **"Front Wheel Lift" ≠ "Manual"**. A manual is a different, harder skill (sustained balance on the rear wheel while rolling). Beginner cards teach front wheel lifts. Advanced cards teach manuals. NICA-aligned terminology.

## Liability principles built into the cards

The cards are liability-conservative throughout:

1. **No specific drop heights, jump dimensions, or feature sizes in writing.** Every feature size is "coach's judgment" — never a number.

2. **Coach demos are explicitly conditional.** Cards say "if you're confident on the feature, demo it. If you're not, don't fake it — walk it with them and discuss." A coach who fakes a demo and crashes is worse than no demo.

3. **Risk-check protocol on Advanced cards.** Three explicit questions before any technical feature: "Can I roll it? What's the consequence if I crash? Have I done this size before?"

4. **One-upmanship language is strong and consistent.** "Stop it immediately" on bigger drops/jumps. "Coach controls feature size, not the rider."

5. **Real medical issues are named.** Osgood-Schlatter (knee pain in growing kids) is called out as a real medical issue, not "tough it out." Concussion symptoms are listed.

6. **The reference page has the deep liability content.** Mandated reporting (Utah DCFS), SafeSport one-on-one rule, hands-off rule, no body comments, photography policy, required training.

## What we chose not to include

A few things considered and rejected:

- **Specific drop/jump heights** — too liability-exposed
- **Doubles or gap jumps at Advanced** — saved for high school NICA
- **Clipless pedals** — flat pedals through Advanced. Clipless creates bad bunny hop patterns and should be a separate conversation with parents
- **Falling Safely beyond Lil' Shredders** — debatable in any age group; kept in LS as developmentally appropriate, not included in older decks
- **Bigger jumps / wood drops / step-ups** — not in any program at this skill ceiling

## What's external (we don't try to be everything)

The curriculum acknowledges what it isn't and points elsewhere:

- **OTB 101 (NICA) — free through Utah High School Cycling League.** Contact Ben at utahmtb.org. This is genuine professional coach training, in-person, for free. **Highest-leverage thing your coaches can do.**
- **Lee McCormack — *Teaching Mountain Bike Skills* book ($29.95).** The actual NICA coach manual.
- **PMBIA Level 1 certification (~$500–700, 3 days).** Gold standard if a head coach wants serious training.
- **SafeSport training, background checks, concussion training.** Required, not optional.

The skill cards are not a substitute for any of these. They're the connective tissue between training and execution.

## What's still rough

Honest assessment of the limitations:

1. **The skill cards have not been field-tested with real coaches.** Everything in them is reasoned from developmental research and verified sources (NICA, Lee McCormack book, Utah League OTB 101 description). But no 17-year-old has actually used them to coach a real session yet. **Test before season starts.**

2. **Lil' Shredders Card 01 (Falling Safely) is not in any verified curriculum.** It's developmentally defensible but not citable. Documented in this summary so future program directors know.

3. **Some pro name-checks may go out of date.** Christopher Blevins, Haley Batten, Puck Pieterse are current. In 5 years, the right names will be different. Plan to refresh.

## How to use this system

**Before the season:**
1. Open `index.html`
2. Read the deck for your program cover-to-cover
3. Read the reference page cover-to-cover
4. Sign up for Utah League OTB 101 (free)
5. Get the Lee McCormack book ($30)

**During the season:**
1. Before each session, re-read the cards for the skills you're teaching
2. When something goes sideways, open the reference page
3. Trust your training. Trust your gut. Trust your coaches.

**End of season:**
1. Note what worked and what didn't
2. Note any drill names or cues that confused coaches
3. Note any safety incidents and how they were handled
4. Feed lessons back into the cards for next year

## The principle underneath everything

The principle the whole curriculum is built on:

> **Warm, consistent, kind coaching beats technically perfect every time. Keep the kids in the sport. The rest follows.**

This applies whether you're teaching a 5-year-old to stop or an 11-year-old to manual. A coach who knows the kids and shows up reliably is worth more than a coach who knows every NICA technique but treats the kids as projects.

The skill cards are a tool. The coaches using them are the real curriculum.
